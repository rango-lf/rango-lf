FIRST YEAR-SPECIFIC ADVICE

First Year Survival Guide
  • The UofT engineering program is difficult, not impossible. But you will need to mind your mindset, as my friend Michael writes about in the linked page. The difference between struggling and drowning often comes down to how you talk to yourself about the struggle.
  • Take APS162: Calculus for Engineers I over the summer. It will allow you to enter the fall semester with one less course on your plate and it prepared me well for the fast pace of my first-year courses. I discuss this course further in this blog post
  • Imposter syndrome is basically a rite of passage here. I felt like I didn't deserve to be here--like I was some kind of underachiever who'd somehow slipped through the cracks while everyone around me was effortlessly brilliant. Guess what; they were all thinking the same thing about themselves.
  • You are not alone. I cannot stress enough how supportive the Skule (TM) community is here. I've never been turned down when I've asked for help from peers in my classes and tutorials, upper-years in Discord servers, and Alumni who have long since graduated. Engineering is a team sport.
  • In university, the onus is on you to reach out for support when you're struggling. Don't wait until you're feeling the pinch to seek help! The First Year Office advertises their resources heavily, and I can confirm that the academic advisors, academic review sessions, and counselling referrals have been incredibly helpful thus far.
  • You will likely need to redefine what success looks like for you. Most of us came in with high school grades in the 90s, which is usually unrealistic to expect in a rigorous program like this.
    • Maybe success is passing all your courses. Maybe it's maintaining your hobbies. Maybe it's just not having a breakdown. Whatever it is, it doesn't have to look like everyone else's definition, but you must be honest with yourself on whether or not those expectations are achievable. Having your name on the Dean's List is not worth your sanity.
  • You need to learn to believe in yourself. You need to truly believe that you can do hard things. As cliche and easier-said-than-done as it sounds, this is the best thing you can do for yourself.
    • My linear algebra teacher, Professor Shai Cohen, once told us that every student he's met has an inner critic inside of their head that tells them "you can't do it", "you're stupid", "you don't deserve to be here". He believes that this mindset is what causes the bulk of students' academic struggles, and I can attest to the fact that if you walk into an exam thinking you're going to fail, you likely will.
    • Building self-confidence is a long, tough battle that I myself am still fighting. It gets easier everyday; the hard part is getting up every single day and pushing through it. But it does get easier(!).
    • If you think you have no idea what you're doing and can't talk yourself into "I can"--lie, lie, and lie to yourself until it is true. Fake it till you make it isn't just a platitude; it's a legit strategy.
  • Grades are not the end-all, be-all. It is always, without fail, a massive reality check to first-year students when they get their midterms back and receive a grade under 60. This is to be expected. This is normal. And this is not the end of the world.
    • "Truss" (haha) the curve. A 55 might literally be above average in some courses. Check the class statistics before you spiral.
    • One bad grade—or even one bad semester—does not define your entire academic career. You have time to recover, and people do it all the time.
    • Your mental and physical health are worth more than a few extra percentage points. Burning yourself out for a marginally better GPA is a losing strategy in the long run.
    • Many successful upper-year students and professional engineers I know barely scraped by in first year. Academic performance and real-world competence are not perfectly correlated.
  • Go outside and touch grass. Join clubs, attend events (there are plenty hosted by EngSoc and discipline clubs, for example), go to career/program fairs, try out a hackathon. These have been the best parts of my UofT experience thus far and they give my brain a much-needed break from grinding.
  • Try to stay healthy. This will look different for everyone, but in general: eat your veggies, drink water, take your meds, practice hygiene, try a new sport, and most importantly, get enough sleep!
    • Elaborating on the sleep part, please do not use caffeine as a substitute for sleep. You are going to screw yourself in the long run by depending on energy drinks to make it through the day.

Commuting
  • Check out the Skule Commuter Students Directorship for more resources and tips! Most of the ones below are taken straight from their site.
  • Try to be productive on your commute (given you don't get motion sick easily). If your commute is longer than 40 minutes, you might want to do some light work, read, or catch up on sleep.
  • Practice your commute route before school starts so you know how long it takes, where you need to transfer, and any short-cuts you can take.
  • Not always possible, but try to avoid rush hours from 7:30-9:30 AM or 4:30-6:30 PM.
  • Arrive at your destination at least 5-10 minutes before class starts. You can plan this out with the Transit app, Google Maps, or Apple Maps.
  • If you are commuting from Queen's park, try to walk through Medical Science Building which will save you a solid 2-3 minutes each time.
  • After your initial payment using your PRESTO card (for TTC, GO, and most local transit systems) you can travel free of cost for the next two hours.
  • If you live close to the UofT Mississauga campus, you can take a shuttle bus between there and St.George. Rides are $7 with a $650 semester pass.
  • For the GO Trains:
    • GO Train schedules are usually very accurate, and doors close 1-2 minutes before departure.
    • If you are travelling from Oakville, Brampton, or Milton, a GO train is likely your best method of commute.
    • Get off at Union Station, and take Line 1 up to Queen's Park Station.
    • Don't forget to set a default trip. If your PRESTO card has a default trip set for travel between two stations, you only have to tap on it. If you take a trip that begins or ends at a different station, you have to tap on and off.
      • If you forget to tap off at the end of your trip, you will be charged the fare to the last stop on the line. You can always appeal a refund by filling out the webform on gotransit.com.
  • For the TTC:
    • TTC has completely inconsistent schedules that are (often) delayed by traffic, weather, etc.
      • TTC trains are usually every 2-3 mins during rush hour and 4-5 mins during off-peak hours.
      • I recommend downloading an app, such as the TTC Watch for Toronto, and using the official TTC website for real-time tracking and updates.
    • If you are travelling from Kipling, Vaughan, or North York, the TTC subway is likely your best method of commute.
    • There are four subway stations on or very near the University of Toronto: Queen's Park Station, Museum Station, St. George Station and Spadina Station. Queen's Park is the the closest to the Engineering Buildings.
    • If you are commuting from the Finch side (Line 1), you can walk to campus from College station (about a 12-minute walk). This may actually save you time compared to riding the subway all the way to Queen's Park (thanks Michael).
